Getting a service member to seek professional help for mental health

Drawing from the goldsmith’s (2004) theory, researchers try to answer some questions about the dilemmas that family members face when they are talking with the returning service members [SMs] about seeking professional help for PTSD. Also, they are studying the ways family members are approaching their SMs and encourage them to seek professional mental help if they are experiencing issues integrating with their normal daily life. They are trying to address these two questions: first, what dilemma do family members experience when talking with service members [SMs] about seeking mental healthcare? Second, what strategies do family members recommend using when attempting to navigate these dilemmas?

The study procedure included eighty family members of U.S. service members [SMs] filling out an anonymous survey. The SMs served in Iraq or Afghanistan and the family members were asked how they would respond to scenarios where their SM was experiencing some sort of mental illness including depression, post-traumatic stress or overall difficulty integrating back into civilian life. Family members described their goal of outcome when talking to their SM, difficulties they may face, and advice they would give to other families with SMs. “Dilemma” was defined as situations where a family member’s communication was misconstrued by the SM, and four dilemmas that frequently came up when talking about mental health included: “(a) getting [the SM] to recognize the problem without implying [they’re] not normal, (b) convincing [the SM] to seek help without implying [they’re] weak, (c) being persistent but patient, and (d) wanting [the SM] to open up without implying [the family member] can understand”. Furthermore, the family members discussed how they managed these dilemmas, which encourages better support for military families and policy changes that acknowledge the deep-seated cultural problem of mental health stigmas.

The analysis also revealed four groups of strategies that family members may use to manage dilemmas. First is When to talk, and this strategy focuses on making choices about when and how often to bring up help-seeking and how much to say about the topic to keep SMs from withdrawing or avoiding. The second is how to talk, and in this strategy family members must communicate caring and commitment along with being polite and showing respect. They also must listen to SMs nonjudgmentally. The third is how to frame it and this strategy focuses on how to frame the talk positively and cooperatively to encourage SMs to seek help as becoming healthy, happy, and productive and in this case SM and family members will seek help together to show that the SM does not have to face the issue alone. The last strategy is where else to turn, and this strategy focuses on seeking help from a third party to speak with the SM about his or her issues. This involves another SM or veteran to either persuade or encourage him or her to seek help. Sometimes this third party could involve religious resources such as a place or person to turn when seeking help or turning to God as a third party and praying with the SM on the issue.

This finding is supporting our previous work on social determinants of health and shows the challenges that a person has in communicating the issue, especially when the issue is regarding mental or physical health. In this article, family members look to find a way to communicate with their service members by implementing one or a combination of the strategies mentioned previously and encourages them to seek professional help for their issue. We mentioned earlier in the social determinants of health about interpersonal communication, which was one of the challenges that patients face during an occurrence of an illness or disease. In conclusion, when a health problem arises, either physical or mental, if the patient does not feel ready to seek professional help, sometimes family members or friends should find a way to encourage the patient to seek help at a right time without implying weakness or pressure. This process needs patience and persistence to reach the desired goal.



Four Dilemmas of Talking with Service Members (SMs) About Seeking Professional Help
Dilemma Connection to Help Seeking Relevant Cultural Beliefs Shaping Meanings Potentially Incongruent Meanings
Getting you to recognize the problem without implying you're not "normal" There's no need to seek help if there's no problem in the first place Stigma about mental illness (damaged, time bomb, beyond help) You're normal but you're not acting normal
Convincing you to seek help without implying you are weak There's no need to seek help if problems will go away over time or the SM can handle it without outside help Military culture: help seeking = weakness, failing to support mission Gender/masculinity: men handle their own problems You need help to regain your strength but asking for help means you are weak
Being persistent but also patient "Denial" means that repeated efforts/conversations likely will be required Never give up on your family/loved one Push but don't be pushy
Wanting you to open up without implying I understand May be a prelude to encouraging the SM to seek help or an alternative to persuasion attempts Importance of "open" communication, feeling understood Necessity of direct experience for understanding (military/civilian divide) I'm trying to understand but I can't understand you
