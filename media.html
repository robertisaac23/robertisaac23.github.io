PTS in the Media

PTS is a mental health condition that is often seen portrayed in the media in a serious and educational light. Let’s not confuse PTS (a condition that is present for an estimated month) with PTSD (a disorder that is present for a longer duration of time). These days, mental health has been a widely recognized topic and luckily has received notable coverage across many outlets. Some of these outlets are social media, news articles, television shows and movies, and website/blog dedications to the many different types of mental health illnesses. PTS is seen to be a part of these many different outlets, being explained. Although, there is advances in media coverage, there is also room for improvement and inclusion of different demographics as well.

The media does a good job in defining what PTS actually is as articles explain that it is, “a common, normal, and often adaptive response to experiencing a traumatic or stressful event” (Bender, 2018). The problem with the media portrayal is that oftentimes, PTS is seen as a violent mental health disorder due to the associations that PTS has with war and focus on uncommon symptoms such as depression, flashbacks, and nightmares (Newman, 2018). This creates a fear within the readers minds of those who are dealing with PTS, as well as confusion for individuals looking for clarity in the symptoms. Personally, I have fallen victim to this exact thinking as well when doing research on the topic. In addition to its negative portrayal, articles in the media about PTS have begun to include less entries about survival and treatment options, which has deteriorated the optimism of those who are looking to overcome PTS (Newman, 2018). While doing research, I became apparent to me that treatment seemed to be scarce and as a reader, even I felt a lack of encouragement. Looking less into the negative aspects of media portrayal, we can see how the media has allowed for the conversation to start and create a platform for regular civilians (not only veterans) to speak about their own experiences.

As mentioned before, media sources often associate PTS with veterans and older adults. This misconception that only veterans get PTS has created a separation for those who do not fall into this category who are currently dealing with PTS. This includes sexual assault victims, trumatic accident surviviors, abuse etc. In addition, a demographic that doesn’t seem to be highlighted often in the media is PTS in children. PTS in children can occur when they are exposed to traumatic events such as sexual/physical/emotional abuse, tragic accidents, or even death. The numbers show that on average children face different types of traumatic events that can lead to the development of PTS and it is as follows:

  • 65% neglect
  • 18% physical abuse
  • 10% sexual abuse
  • 7% psychological (mental) abuse
    • (US Department of Veteran Affairs, 2019)

These different traumatic events can contribute to PTS in children and teenagers, however, can be hard for them to find resources due to the neglect that this demographic faces in the light of PTS.

In addition to the demographic of children being disregarded, it appears that another group of individuals that relate to children are also being pushed aside. An often disregarded population of PTS is that of parents, those who have children and are suffering from the effects of the disorder. Parents with PTS are occasionally seen displaying negative effects towards children such as hitting, yelling, or irritability (Christie, 2019). Another downside to PTS is the idea that PTS has caused a shift in the activities that parents are able to do with their children. Some parents may find it difficult to participate in activities such as going to the movies, being around noisy activities, going to shopping centers, etc (Christie, 2019). These effects of PTS can create a hostel environment for families that parents aren’t necessarily always aware of. What they often are aware of is their love for their children.

The correlation between PTS and minorities are also a subject that the media does not showcase to the public. Minorities can face numerous amounts of stress due to racial injustices and as the article by Monnica T. Williams explains, “When traumatization is due to experiences of racism it is sometimes called racial trauma. Racial trauma can result from major experiences of racism such as workplace discrimination or hate crimes, or it can be the result of an accumulation of many small occurrences, such as everyday discrimination and microaggressions” (Williams, 2019). I personally can account for a time where I was discriminated against at a store and it caused me to have mild PTS whenever I’d recall that moment. What this means for minorities is that the stress they receive from these injustices in the workplace, public, and even home can create the foundation for PTS to be present. A concept that the media seems to disregard and has created a difficult outlet for minorities to receive assistance.

The media has presented PTS in an educational light where it has allowed individuals the ability to search up the meaning of what the term is, symptoms associated with the term, and personal accounts of others with the same diagnosis. The media has been a useful tool and with further additions such as the different demographics, definitions, and expanding on experiences the term PTS in the media can be further developed into a resourceful term for a wide variety of individuals.
